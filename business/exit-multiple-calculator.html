The Moats — What Buyers Actually Pay For
Moat hierarchy, most durable first. Everything below is what a strategic acquirer like LeagueApps, Stack Sports, or TeamSnap cannot build in time — which is why they have to buy instead.
MOAT 1 — FOUNDATIONAL STRUCTURAL
AI-Native Cross-Domain Architecture
What it is
A unified data schema across every business domain — acquisition, sales, retention, revenue, content — running on agent-native infrastructure built for LLM reasoning from day one. The acquisition agent knows which ad drove a lead. The sales agent knows which leads converted. The revenue agent knows which members are at risk. The strategy agent connects it all into one business picture. Every workflow is designed for autonomous agent action and cross-domain reasoning, not retrofitted onto a CRUD app with AI bolted on.
Why an acquirer needs it
Incumbents in sports-tech (LeagueApps, TeamSnap, Stack Sports, Mindbody) all run as siloed modules on legacy architectures. Retrofitting cross-domain intelligence requires redesigning the data model — a multi-quarter rebuild. TeamSnap proved this with their 24-month TeamSnap ONE rewrite in 2025, which was primarily about unlocking the architectural capacity for AI. Even after that rebuild, they face the next problem: getting their data model to actually support cross-domain agent reasoning. That's the gap FullControl already closed. Buyers acquire to skip the multi-year rebuild cycle and inherit the integrated system pre-built.
Impact on multiple
+2–5x on the baseline multiple as the foundational premium for agent-native architecture combined with cross-domain data integration. This is the structural core of the entire moat stack — every other moat depends on it.

Two different durability curves sit underneath this premium:

AI-native portion (roughly +1–2x of the total): Has a window. Incumbents are rebuilding now. This portion is highest today and compresses over the next 18–36 months as more platforms ship their rebuilds.

Cross-domain schema portion (roughly +1–3x of the total): Structurally durable. Incumbents can add AI layers in months; unifying siloed data models across acquisition, sales, retention, revenue, and scheduling is a multi-year project that many companies never actually complete. Stack Sports alone has 35+ acquisitions with incompatible data models — unifying them is a political and technical nightmare. No obvious decay window.

Net effect: exit timing matters for the full premium, but even in a late-cycle exit (2028+), the cross-domain portion should still command a meaningful structural premium.

MOAT 2 — LONG-TERM FLYWHEEL
Proprietary Data Flywheel
What it is
Agent interaction logs, knowledge-gap tables, and longitudinal behavioral data on how sports training businesses actually run — captured at the agent layer from day one. Each new customer deepens the dataset, which improves agent outputs, which attracts more customers.
Why an acquirer needs it
Cannot be replicated without years of live customers operating on the platform. Buyers use this data to train future models, improve products across their entire portfolio, and lock in switching costs at the data layer — the most durable form of competitive advantage in AI.
Impact on multiple
+1–3x, scales with dataset depth. At acquisition time, depth is primarily a function of months live and number of academies operating on the platform.

MOAT 3 — COMPOUNDING NETWORK
Expert Operator Network
What it is
FullControl's agents are trained by a growing network of top-performing academy operators — not just the founders. Elite operators contribute their decision-making patterns, playbooks, and judgment calls into the product layer, where they are continuously refined. The agents put this knowledge to work two ways: they take optimal autonomous actions where the pattern is clear (re-engagement, pricing nudges, campaign adjustments, scheduling), and they provide advisory counsel where nuance is required. Scope covers the five highest-leverage business domains: pricing, sales, marketing, overall strategy, and content.
Why an acquirer needs it
The default way to build vertical AI is "hire three domain experts, have them write a playbook, wire it into prompts." A competitor can do that in a month. FullControl's approach is structurally different: we run an active expert network that continuously refines the agents. That creates a self-reinforcing loop — the best operators want to work with the smartest platform, and the smartest platform is the one the best operators are training. A competitor cannot catch up by hiring three people; they would have to build a rival expert network, which takes years of trust and platform adoption to assemble. Critically, the knowledge is captured in the product layer, not in any individual's head — so an acquirer inherits the full asset even after founder retention expires, and the network-training mechanism travels cleanly to adjacent sports where BAM's basketball brand does not.
Impact on multiple
+1–2x on the baseline multiple as a direct AI-quality premium, plus an additional +10–20% for the institutional-knowledge asset. Durability is high: the network-of-operators mechanism is vertical-agnostic, unlike founder-specific domain expertise which doesn't transfer.

MOAT 4 — RETENTION LOCK-IN
Operational Stickiness
What it is
Once FullControl runs an academy's lead gen, sales, scheduling, retention, billing, and reporting end-to-end, switching platforms means rebuilding operations from scratch. The more systems FullControl touches (Stripe, GHL, Meta, scheduling, membership), the higher the switching cost.
Why an acquirer needs it
High switching costs translate directly into predictable revenue and low churn — the two metrics acquirers use as multiple expanders. A deeply embedded platform is far more valuable than a feature-rich one that customers can leave easily. Unlike timing-based moats, stickiness does not decay — once established, it persists through the acquirer's ownership period.
Impact on multiple
+20–30% via improved retention metrics (GRR, NRR). Indirect impact through reduced churn risk — acquirers apply steep discounts for high-churn businesses, so avoiding the discount is its own premium.

Bessemer State of the Cloud 2025 (switching-cost analysis); Tien Tzuo "Subscribed: Why the Subscription Model Will Be Your Company's Future"; ChartMogul SaaS Retention Benchmarks 2024; SaaS Capital "Switching Costs and Valuation Multiples" (2024).
MOAT 5 — FORCING FUNCTION
Market Takeover Velocity (PMF + Comprehensiveness)
What it is
FullControl captures the training-academy market rapidly because of two stacked advantages. First, product-market fit: once an academy owner sees the product, it matches the way they actually run their business — not a generic SaaS template retrofitted for sports. Second, comprehensiveness: FullControl replaces 4–6 disconnected tools (CRM, scheduling, member management, billing, marketing, reporting) with one integrated system. Onboarding takes an hour, not weeks. Every academy that adopts it rarely leaves and rarely looks elsewhere.
Why an acquirer needs it
The current market is fragmented — academies use LeagueApps for registration, Mindbody for scheduling, Stripe for payments, GHL for marketing, spreadsheets for everything else. No incumbent has a comprehensive vertical product. FullControl does, which means it captures share from everyone simultaneously. If a buyer doesn't acquire FullControl, they watch it compound account-by-account until the vertical is effectively owned — and no competitor can replicate the comprehensiveness without a multi-year rebuild.
Impact on multiple
+10–30% as a strategic urgency premium. This moat's real power is that it amplifies every other moat: strong PMF + comprehensive product = rapid share capture = real competitive tension in a buyer process. It converts "should we acquire?" into "if we don't, who will?" Caveat: this moat decays once the market is captured — it's a growth-phase moat, not a permanent one.

How the math works

Step 1 — Base multiple from growth rate. Growth is the single biggest determinant of SaaS valuation. Piecewise mapping: <20% → 3–5x, 20–40% → 5–7x, 40–80% → 7–10x, >80% → 10–12x.

Step 2 — Add AI-native + cross-domain premium, gated by adoption. A flat additive bump on top of the base, sourced from vertical AI M&A data showing +2–5x above baseline for AI-native architectures. The premium is then gated by the Market Adoption / Moat Validation slider: at 0 (pre-product) the premium realizes at 40%; at 100 (mature, 500+ customers proving every moat) it realizes in full. This models the reality that buyers pay for validated moats, not theoretical ones.

Step 3 — Apply strategic-fit multiplier. Strategic buyers pay 0–40% more than financial buyers for tight capability-gap fills.

Step 4 — Revenue-quality adjustments. NRR above 100% adds, below 100% subtracts. GRR below 90% compresses. Churn above 8% compresses. These are the hard-number proofs of Operational Stickiness (Moat 4).

Step 5 — Risk and diligence compressors. Architecture and IP cleanliness apply bounded multipliers. Data Flywheel (Moat 2) and Expert Operator Network (Moat 3) also apply here — both are gated by the adoption/validation slider, same as the AI premium. A high Flywheel score with low adoption is unrealistic, so the impact is muted until validation rises. This is the central fix: all three technology moats can only realize as much premium as market adoption proves.

Step 6 — Process uplift. Competitive tension adds 8% per additional bidder beyond the first.

Output is presented with a ±20% range because no real deal closes at the modeled number — it's a negotiation, not a formula.
